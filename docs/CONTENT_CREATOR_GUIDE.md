# ModuleForge Content Creator Guide

Welcome to ModuleForge! This guide will help you build interactive financial education modules without any coding knowledge. Think of it like building with blocks — each piece snaps together to create a complete learning experience.

---

## Getting Started

When you first open ModuleForge, you will see a clean workspace divided into two main areas. On the left side is the editor, where you build your module. On the right side is the preview panel, where you can see how your module will look to learners.

### Your First Module

Start by giving your module a name. Click on the title field at the top of the screen that says "Untitled Module" and type in a name, such as "Understanding Home Loans" or "Basics of SIP Investing."

Below the title, you can add a short description. This appears at the top of your module and tells learners what they will learn. For example: "Learn how home loan EMIs work and how to calculate your monthly payments."

### Adding Your First Block

Blocks are the building pieces of your module. Each block is one type of content — a paragraph of text, a picture, a calculator, or a quiz question.

To add a block, look for the big blue button at the bottom of the editor area that says "Add Block." Click it, and a menu will appear showing all 15 block types. Each type has an icon and a short description. Click on any block type to add it to your module.

You can also type a forward slash (/) anywhere in the editor to open the block menu. Start typing the name of the block you want — for example, type "calc" to find the calculator blocks.

### Understanding the Workspace

The editor panel on the left is where you build and arrange content. Each block appears as a card with a thin gray border. When you select a block, it gets a blue border, and a toolbar appears at the top of the block with buttons for moving, duplicating, deleting, and more.

The preview panel on the right updates automatically as you make changes. You can switch between Desktop, Tablet, and Mobile views using the buttons at the top of the screen. This helps you see how your module will look on different devices.

### Saving Your Work

Your work is saved automatically in your browser as you type. You do not need to click a Save button. If you close the browser and come back later, your work will still be there.

When you are ready to save a permanent copy, use the Export button at the top of the screen. This downloads a file to your computer that you can later import to continue working or share with others.

## Block Types Overview

ModuleForge has 15 block types, each designed for a specific purpose. Here is what each one does and when you should use it.

### 1. Rich Text Block

The Rich Text block is your basic text editor. Use it to write explanations, instructions, summaries, or any other text content.

You can format text as bold, italic, underlined, or strikethrough. Create headings of different sizes (H1 through H4) to organize your content. Add bulleted lists, numbered lists, and block quotes. Insert links to websites.

**When to use:** Use this block for almost everything — explaining concepts, giving instructions, summarizing key points, or welcoming learners to your module.

### 2. Image Block

Add pictures to your module using the Image block. You can upload an image from your computer (JPG, PNG, WebP, or SVG, up to 5MB) or paste a URL to an image from the web.

Choose how the image aligns on the page — left, center, right, or full width. Add alt text for accessibility (describes the image for learners who cannot see it) and an optional caption below the image.

**When to use:** Add screenshots of bank websites, charts showing market trends, photos of financial documents, or icons representing concepts.

### 3. Video Embed Block

Add videos from YouTube or Vimeo to your module. Simply paste the video URL, and ModuleForge will automatically detect the platform and show a preview.

The video will appear in a 16:9 widescreen format. In the editor, you will see a thumbnail preview. Learners will see the actual video player.

**When to use:** Embed educational videos about financial concepts, recorded webinars, animated explainers, or video testimonials.

### 4. Quiz (MCQ) Block

Create multiple-choice questions with 2 to 6 options. You can allow one correct answer or multiple correct answers. If you allow multiple correct answers, you can also give partial credit.

Set the number of points for each question, choose how many attempts learners get (1, 2, 3, or unlimited), and decide whether they get feedback immediately after answering or only after submitting the entire quiz.

You can also set a time limit for the question (30, 60, 90, or 120 seconds).

**When to use:** Test understanding of key concepts at the end of a section. For example: "What is the formula for calculating EMI?" with four answer choices.

### 5. Quiz (True/False) Block

Create simple true or false questions. This works just like the MCQ block but with only two options. You can still set points, attempt limits, time limits, and feedback mode.

**When to use:** Quick knowledge checks. For example: "A higher credit score means lower interest rates — True or False?"

### 6. EMI Calculator Block

Add an interactive EMI (Equated Monthly Installment) calculator to your module. Learners can adjust the loan amount, interest rate, and tenure using sliders or by typing numbers.

The calculator shows:
- The monthly EMI amount
- Total interest payable over the loan tenure
- Total payment (principal + interest)
- A pie chart showing the split between principal and interest
- A complete month-by-month amortization schedule showing how each payment is split between principal and interest

**When to use:** Any module about loans — home loans, car loans, personal loans, education loans. Let learners experiment with different loan amounts and tenures to see how it affects their monthly payment.

### 7. SIP Calculator Block

Add a Systematic Investment Plan calculator. Learners enter a monthly investment amount, expected annual return rate, and investment duration in years.

The calculator shows:
- Total amount invested
- Wealth gained (returns)
- Future value of the investment
- A pie chart comparing invested amount vs wealth gained
- A year-by-year growth table showing how the investment grows over time

**When to use:** Modules about mutual fund investing, retirement planning, or long-term wealth building.

### 8. Compound Interest Calculator Block

Add a compound interest calculator. Learners enter the principal amount, annual interest rate, time period, and choose how frequently interest compounds (monthly, quarterly, half-yearly, or annually).

The calculator shows:
- Maturity amount (final value)
- Total interest earned
- A line chart showing growth over time, with one line for the principal and another for the accumulated amount

**When to use:** Modules explaining the power of compounding, long-term savings, or comparing different compounding frequencies.

### 9. Callout Block

Add a highlighted callout box to draw attention to important information. Choose from five styles:
- **Info** (blue) — general information
- **Warning** (yellow) — cautionary notes
- **Tip** (green) — helpful suggestions
- **Important** (purple) — critical points
- **Danger** (red) — serious warnings

Each callout has an icon and rich text content. Use them to make key points stand out from regular text.

**When to use:** Highlight important formulas, warn about common mistakes, provide quick tips, or emphasize key takeaways.

### 10. Divider Block

Add a visual separator between sections of your module. Choose from four styles: solid line, dashed line, dotted line, or a gradient line that fades from transparent to blue. You can also choose the spacing around the divider: compact, normal, or spacious.

**When to use:** Separate different topics within your module, create visual breaks in long content, or mark the transition between sections.

### 11. Accordion/FAQ Block

Create expandable sections that learners can click to reveal content. Each item has a title and content area. Add as many items as you need — but if you go beyond 20 items, you will see a suggestion to consider using fewer.

Learners click on a title to expand the section and click again to collapse it. This keeps your module tidy while still providing detailed information.

**When to use:** FAQ sections, detailed explanations that some learners may want to skip, or organizing related information under expandable headings.

### 12. Progress Tracker Block

Show learners where they are in a multi-step process. Choose between a linear progress bar (a single horizontal bar that fills up) or a branching view (showing each step as a numbered circle, connected by lines).

Enter the labels for each step, and set the current step number to highlight progress.

**When to use:** Visualize the steps in a financial process, such as "Applying for a Loan," showing the stages from application to disbursement.

### 13. Achievement Badge Block

Create an achievement badge that learners can unlock. Choose from 20 different icons, add a title and description, and set an unlock condition. The badge can be locked or unlocked.

When locked, the badge appears grayed out with the unlock condition displayed. When unlocked, it appears in full color with a celebratory feel.

**When to use:** Motivate learners by creating badges for completing sections, scoring well on quizzes, or mastering specific topics.

### 14. Code Snippet Block

Display code examples with syntax highlighting. Choose from 8 programming languages (JavaScript, Python, TypeScript, JSON, SQL, Bash, HTML, CSS). Toggle line numbers on or off.

The code appears in a dark-themed code viewer with the language name displayed at the top and a copy button so learners can copy the code easily.

**When to use:** Show financial calculation formulas, API examples, SQL queries for financial data, or configuration file examples.

### 15. Concept Explainer Block

Create step-by-step explainers with 3 to 10 steps. Each step has a title, description, and a visual indicator (emoji or icon). Enable auto-play to automatically advance through steps at a set interval.

Learners can navigate through steps manually or let the explainer play automatically. Navigation dots at the top show progress through the steps.

**When to use:** Break down complex financial concepts into digestible steps. For example: "How a Stock Trade Works" with steps from placing an order to settlement.

## Using Calculators

Financial calculators are the most powerful feature of ModuleForge. They let learners interact with numbers and see real-time results, making abstract concepts concrete.

### How Learners Use Calculators

In the preview panel and when learners view your published module, calculators work like real interactive tools. Learners can drag sliders, type numbers, or select options, and the results update instantly.

For example, in the EMI Calculator:
1. Drag the loan amount slider to ₹5,00,000
2. Set the interest rate to 9% per year
3. Set the tenure to 60 months (5 years)
4. Instantly see: Monthly EMI of ₹10,379, total interest of ₹1,22,759

Learners can experiment by changing one value at a time to understand how it affects the result. For instance, what happens if they increase the tenure to 120 months? The EMI goes down but the total interest goes up.

### Setting Default Values

When you add a calculator block to your module, you set default values in the editor. These are the starting numbers learners see when they first view the calculator. Choose realistic default values that make sense for your topic.

For example, if you are teaching about home loans, set the default loan amount to ₹30,00,000 (a typical home loan) with a 20-year tenure and 8.5% interest rate. Learners can then adjust these values to match their own situation.

### Charts and Tables

Each calculator shows visual charts that help learners understand the results:
- **EMI Calculator:** A doughnut chart comparing principal vs total interest. An amortization table shows every single month of the loan.
- **SIP Calculator:** A pie chart showing invested amount vs wealth gained. A year-by-year table shows growth over time.
- **Compound Interest Calculator:** A line chart showing growth over the investment period, comparing the principal (flat line) with the accumulated amount (growing curve).

### Teaching with Calculators

Use calculators as teaching tools, not just number crunchers. Here are some ideas:

- **What-if scenarios:** Ask learners to calculate EMI at different interest rates and compare the total interest paid.
- **Goal setting:** Ask learners to figure out how much they need to invest monthly in an SIP to reach a target amount.
- **Comparison:** Compare simple interest vs compound interest by running the same numbers through both calculations.

### Using Calculators to Tell a Story

Financial calculators are most effective when they are part of a narrative. Rather than just presenting a calculator on its own, set up a scenario that learners can relate to.

For example, in a module about buying a home:

"Ravi and Priya are looking to buy their first home. The apartment they like costs ₹45,00,000. They have saved ₹9,00,000 for a down payment and need a loan for the remaining ₹36,00,000. The bank offers a home loan at 8.5% interest. Use the EMI calculator below to find out their monthly payment. Then try different tenures — 10 years, 20 years, and 30 years — to see how the monthly payment changes."

This approach makes the calculator a tool for exploration rather than just a number display. Learners remember the concept better when they discover relationships on their own.

### Using Calculators for Comparisons

Calculators excel at comparisons. Here is an example using the SIP calculator:

Ask learners to calculate what happens when they invest ₹5,000 per month at 12% annual returns for different periods:
- 5 years: Total invested ₹3,00,000, future value approximately ₹4,12,000
- 10 years: Total invested ₹6,00,000, future value approximately ₹11,61,000
- 20 years: Total invested ₹12,00,000, future value approximately ₹49,95,000

The numbers speak for themselves — the power of compounding becomes obvious when learners see it with real numbers they entered themselves.

### Handling Special Cases

The calculators handle unusual situations gracefully:
- If the interest rate is 0%, the calculator shows "EMI = Principal / Tenure" (no division by zero)
- If learners drag sliders rapidly, the calculator waits before recalculating to keep things smooth
- Indian number formatting is used throughout, showing amounts in lakhs (₹1,00,000) and crores (₹1,00,00,000)

## Creating Quizzes

Quizzes help you check whether learners have understood the content. ModuleForge offers two types: Multiple Choice (MCQ) and True/False.

### Setting Up a Quiz Question

When you add a quiz block, you will see the question editor. Start by typing your question in the question field. Then add options.

For MCQ questions, you can have 2 to 6 options. Each option has a text field and a toggle to mark it as correct or incorrect. You can have one correct answer or multiple correct answers.

For True/False questions, you have two fixed options: True and False. Toggle which one is the correct answer.

### Understanding Quiz Settings

Each quiz has several settings you can configure:

**Points:** How many points this question is worth. You can set different point values for different questions.

**Attempt Limit:** How many times a learner can try this question. Choose 1, 2, 3, or unlimited attempts.

**Feedback Mode:** Choose "Immediate" to show the correct answer right after the learner submits, or "Deferred" to only show results at the end of the module.

**Timer:** Set a time limit for the question. Choose 30, 60, 90, or 120 seconds, or no timer at all.

**Allow Multiple Correct:** For MCQ only. If turned on, learners can select more than one option and all correct options must be selected for full credit.

**Partial Credit:** For MCQ with multiple correct answers. If turned on, learners get partial points even if they only select some of the correct options.

### Writing Good Quiz Questions

Here are some tips for creating effective quiz questions:

1. **Test understanding, not memory:** Ask questions that require applying concepts, not just remembering facts.
2. **Make wrong answers plausible:** The incorrect options should be reasonable mistakes a learner might make.
3. **Keep questions clear:** Avoid confusing wording or trick questions.
4. **Explain the answer:** Provide an explanation for why the correct answer is right, especially for True/False questions.
5. **Use real-world scenarios:** Frame questions around realistic financial situations.

### Quiz Scoring

The scoring system tracks each learner's performance across all quizzes in the module. You can set a passing score in the module settings (the gear icon in the top toolbar). If a learner scores below the passing threshold, the module will indicate they need to review the material.

### What If There Is No Correct Answer?

If you create an MCQ question and forget to mark any option as correct, the validation system will warn you about this before you export your module. Always double-check that at least one option is marked correct.

## Arranging Your Module

The order of blocks in your module matters. A well-arranged module guides learners through content logically, from simple to complex, and keeps them engaged.

### Using the Block Toolbar

Each block has a toolbar that appears at the top when you hover over the block or select it. The toolbar gives you quick access to common actions:

- **Grip handle (⠿):** Click and drag to reorder blocks. The cursor changes to a grab hand icon.
- **Up and Down arrows (↑ ↓):** Move the block one position at a time without dragging.
- **Duplicate button (⧉):** Creates an exact copy of the block below the original. The copy gets its own unique ID and starts with the same content and settings.
- **Delete button (🗑):** Removes the block. If you delete by accident, press Ctrl+Z immediately to undo.
- **Lock button (🔒/🔓):** Prevents accidental edits. Locked blocks show a lower opacity and cannot be edited until unlocked.
- **Visibility button (👁/👁‍🗨):** Hides the block from the preview without deleting it. Hidden blocks appear faded in the editor and are skipped in the preview panel.

The toolbar appears only when you hover over or select a block, keeping the interface clean and uncluttered.

### Reordering Blocks

You can rearrange blocks in two ways:

**Drag and Drop:** Click and hold the grip icon (⠿) at the left end of any block's toolbar. Drag the block up or down to its new position. A visual indicator shows where the block will land.

**Arrow Buttons:** Select a block, then click the up arrow (↑) or down arrow (↓) in its toolbar to move it one position at a time.

### Grouping Related Content

Place related blocks together. For example, if you are teaching about home loans:
1. Start with a Rich Text block explaining what a home loan is
2. Follow with an Image block showing a house
3. Add an EMI Calculator block so learners can experiment
4. Include a Callout block with a tip about choosing the right tenure
5. End with a Quiz (MCQ) block to test understanding

### Using Dividers and Headings

Add visual structure to your module with dividers and headings. Use the Rich Text block's heading options (H1, H2, H3) to create section titles. Use Divider blocks between major sections to create clear visual breaks.

### Duplicating and Reusing Blocks

If you need to create several similar blocks, use the Duplicate feature. Select a block and click the duplicate button (⧉) in its toolbar. This creates an exact copy of the block right below it. You can then edit the copy without starting from scratch.

This is especially useful for quiz questions that share similar settings — create one, duplicate it, and change just the question and options.

## Previewing Your Module

The preview panel on the right side of the screen shows you exactly how your module will look to learners. It updates automatically as you make changes in the editor.

### Device Views

Use the device toggle buttons at the top of the screen to switch between three views:
- **Desktop (1440px):** Shows the module at full width, as it would appear on a computer screen.
- **Tablet (768px):** Shows the module at tablet width, useful for iPad and similar devices.
- **Mobile (375px):** Shows the module at phone width, as it would appear on a smartphone.

The preview window animates smoothly when you switch between views, so you can see how the layout adjusts.

### Dark Mode

Click the moon/sun icon to switch between light and dark mode in the preview. This lets learners choose their preferred viewing experience. The editor stays in light mode regardless of the preview setting.

### Interactive Elements

All interactive elements work in the preview:
- Calculators update with real results when you change values
- Charts animate when new data is calculated
- Accordion sections expand and collapse
- Progress trackers show the current step

### Scroll Sync

When you toggle scroll sync (available in the preview settings), scrolling through blocks in the editor automatically scrolls the preview to the matching block. This helps you see how a specific block looks in the preview without searching for it.

## Exporting and Importing

### Exporting Your Module

When your module is ready, export it as a JSON file. Click the "Export" button in the top toolbar. Your browser will download a file named something like "understanding-home-loans-2025-01-15.json".

This file contains everything about your module — all blocks, settings, quiz questions, and content. The file is validated before export to make sure everything is correct.

**What if export fails?** If there is a problem with your module (like a quiz question with no correct answer), the export will fail and show you a list of issues to fix. Check the error messages, fix each issue, and try exporting again.

### Importing a Module

To continue working on a module from a saved file, click the "Import" button and select the JSON file from your computer. The module will open with all blocks, content, and settings restored.

**Important:** When you import a module, the system generates new unique IDs for the module and all blocks. This prevents conflicts if you import the same module multiple times.

### Sharing Modules

Share the exported JSON file with other content creators. They can import it into their own ModuleForge workspace and make changes. This makes it easy to collaborate on module development.

### What About Images?

Images are embedded directly into the exported file. This means the file can be larger, but it also means you never have to worry about broken image links — everything is self-contained in one file.

## Keyboard Shortcuts

Using keyboard shortcuts helps you work faster. Here are all the shortcuts available:

### Block Actions
- **Alt + Up Arrow:** Move selected block up
- **Alt + Down Arrow:** Move selected block down
- **Ctrl + D:** Duplicate selected block
- **Ctrl + Backspace:** Delete selected block
- **Ctrl + L:** Lock or unlock selected block
- **Ctrl + /:** Open block settings
- **Ctrl + Shift + H:** Toggle block visibility (hide/show)

### Navigation
- **Tab:** Move to the next block (shows a 2-pixel outline around the focused block)
- **Shift + Tab:** Move to the previous block
- **Enter:** When a block is focused, enter edit mode
- **Escape:** Exit edit mode and return focus to the block

### Slash Command Menu
- **/:** Open the block type menu (type at the beginning of a block or between blocks)
- **Up/Down Arrow:** Navigate through block types in the menu
- **Enter:** Select the highlighted block type
- **Escape:** Close the menu without adding a block

### Global Shortcuts
- **Ctrl + Z:** Undo your last action
- **Ctrl + Shift + Z:** Redo an action you just undid

## Troubleshooting

### Accidentally Deleted a Block

Do not panic! Immediately press Ctrl + Z (or Cmd + Z on Mac) to undo the deletion. Your block will come back exactly as it was.

If you have made other changes since deleting and cannot undo, you can re-create the block from scratch. If you recently exported your module, import the exported version to get everything back.

### Changes Disappeared

If you close your browser and come back to find your changes missing, it could be because your browser cleared its storage. Always export your module as a JSON file before closing your browser — this gives you a permanent backup you can import later.

The browser saves your work automatically, but clearing browser data (cookies, cache, site data) will remove unsaved modules.

### Export Not Working

If the Export button does not seem to work:

1. Check if your browser is blocking downloads. Look at the top or bottom of your browser window for a download blocked notification.
2. If you see error messages about validation problems, read each message carefully. Common issues include:
   - A quiz question with no correct answer selected
   - A module title that is too long (over 200 characters)
   - A calculator with invalid values (like zero months of tenure)
3. Fix each issue listed in the error messages and try exporting again.

### Import Not Working

If the Import button does not seem to work:

1. Make sure you are selecting a .json file. Other file types will not work.
2. If you see error messages about invalid JSON, the file may be corrupted. Try exporting again from the original source.
3. If you see validation errors, the module may have been created with an older or different version. The errors will tell you what needs to be fixed.

### Preview Looks Wrong

If the preview panel does not look right:

1. Try switching to a different device view and back. This refreshes the preview.
2. Check if the block is hidden — a hidden block has a crossed-out eye icon in its toolbar.
3. If a calculator shows strange results, check the default values you entered in the editor.
4. If the editor panel and preview panel seem out of sync, try toggling the preview off and on using the Preview button.

### Calculator Not Responding

If a calculator slider or input does not seem to work:

1. Make sure you are in the preview panel, not the editor. Calculators are interactive only in preview mode.
2. Try clicking directly on the input field and typing a number instead of dragging the slider.
3. If the chart does not update, try changing a different value first.

### Browser Running Slowly

If ModuleForge feels slow or laggy:

1. Close other browser tabs that may be using memory.
2. If you have many blocks (50+), try reducing the number or splitting your module into multiple modules.
3. Export your work and refresh the browser page.

### Laptop or Desktop Works Best

ModuleForge works in any modern browser, including Chrome, Firefox, Edge, and Safari. However, for the best experience, use a laptop or desktop computer rather than a phone. The split-panel layout (editor on the left, preview on the right) is designed for larger screens. On a phone screen, the panels would be too small to work with comfortably.

### Moving Between Modules

If you need to work on multiple modules, you can switch between them using the Import feature. Export your current module first, then import the other module. When you want to go back, import the first module again. This is also a great way to reuse blocks you created in a previous module — just import the old module, copy the blocks you need to your new module, then import the original module back to continue where you left off.

### Planning Your Module Before You Build

Before you start adding blocks, it helps to plan out your module on paper or in a simple document. Think about what you want learners to learn by the end of the module. Write down the key points you need to cover, in the order that makes the most sense for learning. A typical financial education module follows this structure:

1. **Introduction:** A Rich Text block explaining what learners will learn and why it matters.
2. **Concept Explanation:** A Concept Explainer or more Rich Text blocks breaking down the core ideas step by step.
3. **Interactive Element:** A Calculator block where learners can experiment with numbers hands-on.
4. **Reinforcement:** A Callout block highlighting key takeaways or common mistakes.
5. **Assessment:** A Quiz block to test understanding.
6. **Summary:** A final Rich Text block recapping what was covered and what to learn next.

This structure works well for most financial topics, from loans and investments to budgeting and taxes.

### Browser Tabs

You can have multiple ModuleForge tabs open in your browser, but be careful — each tab maintains its own copy of the module. Changes in one tab will not automatically appear in the other tab. If you make changes in two tabs at the same time, you could lose work. A safer approach is to work in one tab and use Export to save your progress before opening a second tab.

### Need More Help?

If you encounter issues not covered here, contact the EduForge support team. Include:
- A description of what you were trying to do
- What you expected to happen
- What actually happened
- Any error messages you saw

## Glossary

**Accordion:** A block type that shows content in expandable sections that learners can click to open or close.

**Amortization Schedule:** A table showing each loan payment broken down into interest and principal portions over the entire loan term.

**Block:** A single piece of content in your module, such as text, an image, a calculator, or a quiz question.

**Callout:** A highlighted box that draws attention to important information, available in five styles: info, warning, tip, important, and danger.

**Compound Interest:** Interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods.

**Device Toggle:** Buttons at the top of the screen that switch the preview between desktop, tablet, and mobile views.

**EMI (Equated Monthly Installment):** A fixed monthly payment a borrower makes to a lender on a loan.

**Export:** Download your module as a JSON file that can be stored, shared, or imported later.

**Import:** Open a previously exported JSON file to continue working on a module.

**MCQ (Multiple Choice Question):** A quiz question with several answer options where learners select the correct one(s).

**Module:** The complete learning unit you build, containing multiple blocks arranged in order.

**Preview Panel:** The right-side panel that shows how your module looks to learners.

**Rich Text:** Formatted text that can include bold, italic, headings, lists, and links.

**SIP (Systematic Investment Plan):** An investment method where you invest a fixed amount regularly (usually monthly) in mutual funds.

**Slash Command:** Type a forward slash (/) to open the block type menu and quickly add a new block.

**Tenure:** The duration of a loan, usually measured in months or years.
